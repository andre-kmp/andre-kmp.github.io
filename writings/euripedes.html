5 lessons I learned from Eurípedes

July 2026.

Between 2021 and 2022, I was Automation Engineering intern, and our team included a consultant named Eurípedes Nóbrega. At the time, he was a post-doc in engineering (I wouldn't be surprised if he had earned another degree since then) and had solid experience across various engineering fields. Some of the advice he gave the team resembled aphorisms: concise and deeply meaningful about life. I used to write them down.

I am pleased to present some of that advice here. For each one, I include the context in which it was given - disclaimer: recalling this from memory, 5 years later - as well as other reflections it sparks in me.

  1. The lighter, the better.

    From the original in portuguese: "Quanto mais leve, melhor".

    That day, the engineerings were discussing the many trade-offs regarding the product design, and Eurípedes provided this razor. Mass reduction is a powerful form of optimization in engineering.

    This applies well to other areas and other quantities, since weight does not always refer to physical mass - it can be excess of information, friction, volume, and so on. If you are faced with a shortage, add more; but if you are faced with fatigue, there is something to be fixed, or at least, a loss that could be avoided simply by reducing the load.

  2. Refining is much easier than kickstarting.

    From the original in portuguese: "É muito mais fácil aperfeiçoar do que dar um chute inicial".

    He was advising us about the importance of completing a first prototype so we could begin refining it, instead of being stuck planning a flawless version. Eurípedes used to reinforce this point in other words, such as "you are only capable of discovering where to go if you are already somewhere".

    I can think of few things as powerful as finishing stuff. This act shifts the mind from a state of idealization to one of analyzing an external, concrete entity, which reduces anxiety, enhances metacognitive awareness and uncovers new possibilities. "Concluding something" means taking any decision and moving on to a next stage.

  3. One is the same as none.

    From the original in portuguese: "Um é igual a nenhum".

    He spoke this phrase more than once, usually highlighting the need to collect data from more than one device when running tests; after all, if only one device is collecting data, how can you tell if a hardware fault is corrupting data?

    Extrapolating the meaning, this can be also about the importance of having more than one advisor for life's challenges. We never know whether the advice truly stems from an objective analysis of the situation. Talking to more than one person helps identify when it's not the case.

  4. Every system needs to monitor itself.

    From the original in portuguese: "Todo sistema precisa monitorar a si próprio".

    The goal of the system we were working on was to detect future failures in electric motors, and the entire focus was placed on this, as this was how the results would be evaluated. Eurípedes advised the engineers to create a routine that would allow the system to monitor itself. The how-to of this process is a matter of Control Engineering, a fascinating field.

    If we replace "system" with "human being" and "monitor" with "know", we arrive at Socrates and the need for knowing thyself. It is an effort that remains invisible from the outside, yet is fundamental to ensure the well-being of the system as a whole. This "know thyself" concept affects each person differently, and that unique path is the right one for each individual.

  5. Redundancy is important because it creates security.

    From the original in portuguese: "É importante ter redundância porque a redundância gera segurança".

    At that moment, he was explaining the utility of similar results from different tests. Even though it created the impression of repeating the same task, it was an effective indicator of how well we were on the right track and how robust our results were.

    Common sense tends to criticize redundancy, viewing it as a waste of resources. However, it is redundancy that generates information, and its complete absence would result in the lack of any detectable pattern. How to start finding redundancy in the everyday life? Two steps: (i) consistency; (ii) trust the process.

Great engineers tend to have an elegant way of tackling challenges. I believe that the deep rational effort required to solve material problems sharpens intuition and the ability to see the big picture. May the force be with you, colleagues.
